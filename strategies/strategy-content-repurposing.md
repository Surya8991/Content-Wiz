# Content Repurposing System

_Last updated: 2026-05-01_


## Goal and Metric

Multiply the reach and lifespan of every content asset without proportionally increasing production time. Target: each primary content asset (article, webinar, report, case study) generates at least 8 derivative pieces across 3 or more channels within 7 days of the original publication. Repurposing costs substantially less than creating net-new content for every channel, since research, framing, and proof points carry over instead of being rebuilt from scratch.

---

## Core Principles

**1. Create once, distribute many times, adapt for each surface.** Copy-pasting the same text across platforms performs poorly because every platform has a different native format, audience expectation, and engagement mechanic. A LinkedIn post is not a truncated blog post. A newsletter excerpt is not the article introduction. Each derivative piece must feel native to its destination.

**2. The pillar asset is the investment; micro-content is the return.** A long-form article, a research report, a recorded webinar, or a client case study is the asset worth creating with full production effort. Everything else flows from it. The pillar does not exist to fill a calendar; it exists to give the repurposing engine something substantial to work with.

**3. Spend at least as much time distributing as creating.** Ross Simmonds' documented framework: every minute of creation time should be matched by at least one minute of distribution and repurposing. Most teams invert this ratio - 90% creation, 10% distribution - and then wonder why content with strong ideas gets no reach.

**4. Not every piece qualifies for full repurposing.** Repurposing resources should go to assets that signal value first: strong organic traffic, high engagement, strategic relevance to the current sales focus, or original data that has not been fully distributed yet. Repurposing mediocre content at scale produces mediocre reach at scale.

**5. Resharing is not spamming.** On any given day, 80% or more of an audience does not see a given post. Resharing the same insight in a different framing six to eight weeks later reaches the majority of the audience for the first time. Repurposing is serving the audience that was missed, not repeating yourself to the audience that already saw it.

## The Repurposing Map

Every primary asset type flows into a defined set of derivative formats. Use this map as a production checklist.

### Source: Long-form article or blog post (1,500+ words)

| Derivative | Channel | Time to produce |
|---|---|---|
| 3-5 LinkedIn text posts (one insight per post) | LinkedIn | 20 min each |
| 1 LinkedIn carousel (6-10 slides, visual framework from article) | LinkedIn | 45 min |
| 1 newsletter issue (200-word excerpt + CTA to full article) | Email | 20 min |
| 1 Medium republish (identical or lightly edited, 2-week offset from original) | Medium | 15 min |
| 2-3 HARO pitch blocks (extract stat-backed claims for journalist queries) | HARO | 15 min |
| 1 Twitter/X thread (hook + 5-7 points, final tweet links to article) | Twitter/X | 20 min |
| 1 short-form video script (the single most counterintuitive insight, 60-90 sec) | YouTube Shorts / LinkedIn video | 30 min |

**Total derivative pieces: 9-12 from one article. Total additional time: 3-4 hours.**

### Source: Webinar or recorded training session (45-90 min)

| Derivative | Channel | Time to produce |
|---|---|---|
| 1 edited highlight video (5-10 min, best segment) | YouTube | 60 min |
| 3-5 short video clips (60-90 sec each, key moments) | LinkedIn video / YouTube Shorts | 30 min |
| 1 long-form blog post (transcript-based, edited for reading) | Blog / Medium | 90 min |
| 1 downloadable summary guide (key frameworks from the session) | Lead magnet / SEO resource | 60 min |
| 1 newsletter issue (top 3 takeaways + link to recording) | Email | 20 min |
| 3-5 LinkedIn posts (one insight per post, pulled from session Q&A) | LinkedIn | 20 min each |
| Quote graphics (3-5 most quotable lines, branded visual) | LinkedIn / Instagram | 30 min |

**Total derivative pieces: 12-18. Total additional time: 6-7 hours.**

### Source: Client case study (published or internal)

| Derivative | Channel | Time to produce |
|---|---|---|
| 1 detailed written case study page (SEO-optimized, 800-1200 words) | Website / blog | 90 min |
| 1 LinkedIn narrative post (story format: situation, challenge, outcome) | LinkedIn | 25 min |
| 1 newsletter feature (250 words: problem, approach, result) | Email | 20 min |
| Data points extracted for HARO pitches | HARO | 15 min |
| Outcome statistic turned into a LinkedIn carousel slide or visual proof block | LinkedIn | 20 min |
| Quote from client contact (with permission) used as LinkedIn post hook | LinkedIn | 10 min |

**Total derivative pieces: 5-7. Total additional time: 3 hours.**

### Source: Original research or data report

| Derivative | Channel | Time to produce |
|---|---|---|
| 1 full report landing page (gated lead magnet, 800-1500 words) | Website | 2 hours |
| 1 long-form Medium article (key findings narrative, ungated) | Medium | 60 min |
| 5-8 LinkedIn data posts (one finding per post, chartable data as carousel) | LinkedIn | 20 min each |
| 3-5 newsletter issues (one finding per issue over sequential weeks) | Email | 20 min each |
| 5-10 HARO pitch data blocks (each finding usable as a journalist source) | HARO | 30 min total |
| 1 PR pitch to trade publications (news angle from most surprising finding) | Earned media | 45 min |
| 1 SEO article per major finding (each finding becomes a ranked article) | Blog / SEO | 60 min each |

**Total derivative pieces: 20-35 over 4-6 weeks. This is the highest-ROI asset type for a B2B brand.**

## The Production Sequence

The following order minimizes rework and ensures the highest-value derivatives are produced before the asset loses freshness:

**Day 0 (publish day):** Publish the pillar asset. Send the newsletter excerpt. Post one LinkedIn text post pulling the strongest single insight.

**Day 1-2:** Produce the LinkedIn carousel. Post a second LinkedIn text post (different insight, different angle from the same article).

**Day 3-4:** Post the third LinkedIn insight. Submit 1-2 HARO pitches using claims from the article where relevant queries exist.

**Day 5-7:** Publish the Twitter/X thread. Produce the short-form video script. Add to Medium republish queue (2 weeks from original publish date).

**Week 2-3:** Reshare the LinkedIn carousel with a new caption. Post the video short. Publish Medium version.

**Week 6-8:** Reshare one of the top-performing LinkedIn posts from the original asset with a new framing sentence. 80% of the audience did not see it the first time.

## Adaptation Rules by Channel

Each derivative must feel native. These are the key adaptations per channel:

**LinkedIn (from article):** Cut to one insight. Write in single lines. Replace paragraph structure with white space. Add a CTC (call to comment) question at the end. Remove article-specific context that assumes the reader has read the piece.

**Email newsletter (from article):** Extract one insight plus one proof point. Write as a direct message, not a teaser. Include the first 100-150 words of the article as the body of the newsletter, then a clear "read the full piece" link. Do not summarize the entire article in the email.

**Medium republish (from blog):** Publish identically or with minor edits. Add a canonical link pointing to the original URL to protect SEO. Wait 2 weeks minimum after the original to allow the source version to index first.

**Canonical/duplicate-content rule for every cross-posted channel, not just Medium:** Any channel that supports a canonical or rel-alternate mechanism (Dev.to/Hashnode frontmatter `canonical_url`, a WordPress `rel="canonical"` tag, an "originally published at" line) must point back to the original piece's URL. Any channel with no such mechanism (Instagram, GMB, Pinterest, Quora, YouTube description) must carry a substantially rewritten version instead - never a lightly reworded copy of the source.

**HARO pitch (from article):** Extract any original statistics, named case study outcomes, or counterintuitive claims. These become the proof blocks in HARO responses when relevant journalist queries arrive. Maintain a running list of the brand's "pitch-ready data points" updated with each new article.

**Twitter/X thread (from article):** Hook tweet = the single strongest claim from the article. Subsequent tweets = each major section compressed to 1-2 sentences. Final tweet = link to full article. Threads should read as self-contained; the article is optional depth, not required context.

**Short-form video (from article):** Pick the one counterintuitive insight and script it as a monologue: problem in sentence 1, surprising claim in sentence 2, proof in sentences 3-4, practical implication in sentence 5. 60 to 90 seconds. No intro, no outro, no "don't forget to subscribe."

## The Pitch-Ready Data Bank

Maintain a running document (or spreadsheet) that catalogs every original data point, case study outcome, and counterintuitive claim produced by the brand's content. Update it with each new article or report.

Structure each entry as:
- **The claim:** (one sentence, stat or finding)
- **The source:** (article title, date, URL)
- **The context:** (what it means, who it is relevant for)
- **HARO-ready phrasing:** (the exact paragraph used in journalist pitch format)

This bank means any team member can respond to a relevant HARO query in 10 minutes using pre-approved, pre-formatted data points rather than starting from scratch each time.

## Failure Modes

1. **Copy-paste cross-posting.** Publishing the same block of text on LinkedIn, Twitter, and email simultaneously is easily detectable and performs poorly on all three. Platforms surface native content; imported text is algorithmically penalized and intuitively off-putting to readers.

2. **Repurposing without editorial judgment.** Not every paragraph of an article is worth turning into a LinkedIn post. The repurposing map defines what to create; editorial judgment decides which specific insights are strong enough to stand alone as posts. Weak extracts produce low engagement and train the algorithm to suppress the account.

3. **Treating repurposing as a task rather than a system.** Repurposing works when it is built into the production workflow, not added as an afterthought after the article is published and attention has moved on. The derivative plan should be drafted at the same time as the original piece.

4. **No resharing cadence.** Teams that repurpose on publish day and never touch the asset again leave most of the potential reach uncaptured, since the majority of an audience misses any single post. Schedule resharing dates at 6 weeks and 12 weeks for the best-performing derivatives.

5. **Losing the original source in the derivative.** Every derivative piece should link back to the original pillar asset wherever the platform allows. This drives traffic to the brand's owned channel and signals topical authority to search engines through consistent internal reference patterns.

## Adaptation for Your Brand

The repurposing system connects across the brand's content channels (HARO, Medium, LinkedIn, SEO blog, email newsletter, and any other channel this tool supports, including case studies, guest articles, podcast guesting, and YouTube) into one production loop. A single original research finding becomes: a ranked SEO article, three LinkedIn posts, a newsletter issue, a Medium essay, and two HARO pitch data points - all within 10 days of original publication, with no new research required. Brand voice rules apply to each derivative independently: no em dashes in any format, plain paragraph structure for email and article derivatives, single-line structure for LinkedIn derivatives, stat-backed claims with named sources wherever a statistic appears. The pitch-ready data bank is the most immediately valuable asset to build first - it makes the HARO channel faster, the LinkedIn proof system stronger, and the newsletter more data-dense from the first week of implementation.
