# Partnership and Co-Marketing Strategy

_Last updated: 2026-07-14_

## Goal and Metric

Run co-branded campaigns and joint content with partner companies that expand reach into each partner's audience without favoring one brand over the other. Target: each co-marketing campaign reaches a combined audience at least 1.5x either partner's individual list size, with a lead-share agreement that delivers each partner at least 100 net-new qualified contacts per campaign.

---

## Core Principles

**1. Both brands must be confirmed and represented before any content is generated.** Co-marketing content requires detecting and confirming both partner brands independently (name, description, audience, voice) before writing anything. Content that defaults to one brand's voice or omits the other partner's details is not usable and damages the partnership relationship.

**2. Balance is a design constraint, not a negotiation outcome to litigate per asset.** Neither partner's logo, name, or CTA should dominate the other's in shared assets. Agree on the balance rule once at the start of the partnership (equal logo size, alternating first-mention order across assets, equal CTA prominence) rather than re-negotiating balance on every individual piece of content.

**3. The partnership must serve the shared audience's actual need, not just each brand's promotional goal.** The strongest co-marketing content addresses a real problem that sits at the intersection of both partners' offerings (a workflow that requires both products, a challenge both audiences share) rather than two separate promotional messages bolted together under one headline.

**4. Audience overlap and complementary fit determine partnership value, more than partner size.** A smaller partner whose audience closely matches the target buyer profile produces better campaign results than a larger partner with only tangential audience overlap. Evaluate potential partners on audience fit first, brand prestige second.

**5. Clear ownership of each deliverable prevents the most common co-marketing failure mode: nothing shipping.** Co-marketing projects stall when it is unclear which partner owns drafting, which owns design, and which owns distribution for each asset. Assign a single owner per deliverable at kickoff, even when both partners contribute content.

---

## Strategy Architecture

**Step 1: Identify and vet the partner on audience fit and complementary offering.** Confirm the partner's audience genuinely overlaps with the target buyer, and the partner's offering is complementary rather than competitive with the brand's own product.

**Step 2: Agree on the balance rule and deliverable ownership before drafting any content.** Set the logo/mention/CTA balance convention and assign a single owner per asset (co-authored article, joint announcement, co-branded campaign copy) before content work begins.

**Step 3: Build content per `Partnership_Comarketing_Prompt.txt`'s three formats.** Co-Authored Content (a jointly-bylined article or resource), Joint Announcement (a partnership launch announcement), Co-Branded Campaign Copy (ongoing campaign assets referencing both brands).

**Step 4: Route each asset through both partners' brand and legal review before publishing.** Both partners need sign-off on any content using their brand name, logo, or claims about their product, not just the partner who drafted it.

**Step 5: Distribute through both partners' channels with agreed lead-sharing mechanics.** Define in advance how leads generated by the campaign are shared or split between partners, and instrument tracking (UTM parameters, dedicated landing pages) before launch so attribution is unambiguous after the fact.

**Step 6: Report combined results to both partners and use them to inform the next campaign.** Share reach, engagement, and lead numbers with the partner regardless of which side performed better, to sustain trust for future co-marketing work.

---

## Cadence

- Partner identification and vetting: ongoing, prioritized against current campaign themes
- Balance rule and ownership agreement: set once per partnership, revisited only if the partnership scope changes materially
- Content production: per campaign, typically 2-4 weeks lead time for co-authored assets
- Joint results reporting: within 2 weeks of campaign completion
- Partnership relationship review: quarterly for ongoing partnerships

---

## Failure Modes

1. **Content defaults to one brand's voice or omits the other partner's confirmed details.** This is the most common failure and it is avoidable by confirming both brands before drafting, per Principle 1.
2. **Imbalanced logo, mention, or CTA treatment favoring one partner.** Even unintentional imbalance reads as disrespect to the disadvantaged partner and erodes the relationship for future campaigns.
3. **No single owner assigned per deliverable, so nothing ships on schedule.** Shared ownership without a named lead produces stalled projects more often than it produces genuinely collaborative ones.
4. **Partnering on audience overlap that does not actually exist.** A campaign built on assumed audience fit that turns out to be weak produces poor results for both partners and undermines the case for future co-marketing.
5. **No agreed lead-sharing or attribution mechanism set before launch.** Ambiguity about which partner "owns" a lead generated by joint content creates disputes after the campaign that damage the relationship regardless of campaign performance.

---

## Recommended Tools

- **Shared campaign brief document (Notion, Google Docs):** single source of truth for balance rules, deliverable ownership, and timeline, accessible to both partners.
- **UTM tagging and a shared analytics dashboard:** unambiguous attribution of campaign-driven traffic and leads to each partner's channel.
- **Dedicated co-branded landing page:** a shared conversion point that displays both brands equally and captures leads with clear attribution to the joint campaign.
