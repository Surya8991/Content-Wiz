# HARO / Journalist Source Strategy

_Last updated: May 2026_


## Goal and Metric

Win expert quote pickups in Tier 1 and Tier 2 publications that generate backlinks, brand mentions, and EEAT authority signals. Target: 15% or higher pickup rate per query responded to. Treat this as a stretch goal calibrated against your own results over time rather than a fixed industry number - pickup rates vary widely by niche, query volume, and response speed.

---

## Core Principles

**1. Speed is the first filter, quality is the second.** Journalists stop reading once they have enough good material. Responding within 30 to 60 minutes of the email alert arriving puts the pitch in the first wave reviewed. After 4 hours, the odds drop sharply.

**2. The first sentence is a trust checkpoint, not a greeting.** Journalists decide whether to keep reading based on the opening credential. Skip pleasantries. State who you are and why that makes the answer credible before making any claim.

**3. Write for the journalist's reader, not for the brand.** Every HARO pitch that mentions products, services, or pricing signals that the source is trying to extract value, not provide it. Journalists delete these. The only purpose of the pitch is to make the journalist's article stronger.

**4. Deliver the answer, not a conversation starter.** A response ending in "happy to discuss further" or "contact me for details" fails. The pitch must be the complete, publishable answer. Journalists do not have time to chase clarifications from every source.

**5. HARO is a relationship channel, not a link-harvesting channel.** Sources who tag journalists when articles go live, who pitch consistently within their expertise, and who respond to follow-ups quickly get invited back. The first pickup opens the door; behavior after it determines whether the door stays open.

## Repeatable Pitch Structure

**Subject line**
Format: `[Credential or Title] on [Query Topic]`
Example: `Corporate Training Director on Why Upskilling Budgets Stall in Q4`
Keep it under 10 words. The subject line is the first credibility signal, not a headline.

**Block 1: Credential (2 sentences, 25-40 words)**
Name, title, company, one quantified claim that establishes authority on the specific topic. No adjectives. No superlatives. Verifiable facts only.
Example: `Ravi Mehta is the Head of Corporate L&D at [Your Company], where the team has designed and delivered skills assessments for over 10,000 employees across 35 countries.`

**Block 2: Direct Answer (2-3 sentences, 40-70 words)**
Answer the query question exactly, in plain language, written to be quoted. Bold the single most quotable sentence. Avoid jargon unless the publication is trade-specific.
Example: `**The most common reason upskilling budgets stall is that training is still positioned as a cost center rather than a performance lever.** When L&D teams tie program outcomes to revenue metrics like sales cycle speed or client retention, budget approval rates rise significantly.`

**Block 3: Proof (2-3 sentences, 30-60 words)**
One concrete data point, named example, or before-and-after result that makes the claim checkable. First-party data from the brand's own work outperforms third-party statistics. If citing a study, name it explicitly.
Example: `Across 200 enterprise training engagements tracked last year, organizations that linked L&D spend to a specific KPI secured 40% larger training budgets in the following cycle than those that did not.`

**Sign-off (3-4 lines)**
Full name, title, company, URL, phone (optional). One sentence offering to share the article when published. No call to action, no pitch for a follow-up interview.
Example:
```
Ravi Mehta
Head of Corporate L&D, [Your Company]
https://yourcompany.com
[phone optional]
Happy to promote this article to our audience once it goes live.
```

**Total pitch length: 150 to 280 words.** Below 150 reads thin. Above 300 reads like a sales proposal.

## Hook Patterns That Win Pickup

These five opening angles consistently outperform generic intros across the top-performing HARO contributors:

1. **The number opener:** `After working with [specific number] companies on [topic], the pattern that consistently appears is...` Signals scale of experience before making the claim.

2. **The counterintuitive opener:** `Most [audience] assume [common belief]. The data points to the opposite.` Gives the journalist a headline-worthy angle immediately.

3. **The specific scenario:** `In a [recent/specific] engagement with a [company type] in [industry], the root cause turned out to be...` Shows first-hand observation, not textbook knowledge.

4. **The single-sentence answer:** Lead with the most quotable sentence possible before any credential. Journalists sometimes lift the first sentence directly. If it is strong enough, the credential reads as confirmation rather than gate.

5. **The cost of inaction:** `The consequence of [common mistake] is [quantified result]. Here is why that happens and how to avoid it.` Frames the answer around stakes, which is what readers actually want.

## Proof System

Ranked by persuasiveness to journalists:

- **First-party data** from the brand's own programs, clients, or research (highest)
- **Named client outcome** with specific metric and timeframe
- **Named third-party study** with year and publishing body
- **Industry report statistic** cited with source
- **Personal observation** framed as pattern across specific number of cases (lowest, but still valid)

Never use vague qualifiers like "many companies" or "studies show." Name the study or remove the claim.

## Distribution Loop

1. Set a Google Alert for the brand name and each contributor's name.
2. When a pickup goes live, share the article on LinkedIn with a tag to the journalist (not just the outlet).
3. Add the mention to a media page or bio within 48 hours.
4. Repurpose the published quote in blog content, proposals, or social proof sections on the website.
5. Reply to the journalist's next query on the same topic, referencing the previous coverage. This builds a recurring source relationship.

---

## Cadence

- Subscribe to HARO / Featured / Connectively email alerts and relevant niche digests.
- Aim to respond to 3 to 5 queries per week, not per day. Volume without relevance tanks pickup rate.
- Only respond to queries where the brand has genuine first-hand expertise. Off-topic pitches waste the journalist's time and damage the sender's credibility.
- Block 30 minutes after each HARO email arrives to filter and respond while the queue is still short.

---

## Failure Modes

1. **Over-length pitches (300+ words).** Journalists are reading dozens of responses. A long pitch signals that the source cannot communicate efficiently.

2. **Self-promotion in the response body.** Any mention of products, services, pricing, or "learn more" links triggers deletion. The only link allowed is the company URL in the sign-off.

3. **Generic answers available via a Google search.** If the answer contains no original data, no personal experience, and no specific example, it adds nothing the journalist could not find faster elsewhere.

4. **Responding to off-topic queries.** A marketing director answering a query about tax accounting simply because the deadline is close damages credibility with the journalist and wastes a pitch from the daily limit.

5. **AI-generated responses without personal perspective.** Journalists actively flag and ignore responses that lack authentic voice, specific examples, or verifiable first-hand experience. A pitch that reads like a summary paragraph from a generic article will not be picked.

---

## Adaptation for Your Brand

Brand voice rules from the project memory apply directly here. Use no em dashes in any section of the pitch. Write credential blocks and proof blocks in plain, declarative paragraph form (no bullet points in the credential block itself). Back every claim with a statistic sourced from brand data or a named third-party study. Because HARO pitches are attributed to a named expert rather than written in first person on behalf of the brand, the no-first-person-pronoun rule applies at the organizational voice level: the pitch is attributed to a specific named person and written in third person in the credential block, then switches to direct expert voice in the answer block only when quoting that expert directly.
