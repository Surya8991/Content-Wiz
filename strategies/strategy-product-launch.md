# Product Launch Strategy

_Last updated: 2026-07-13_

## Goal and Metric

Execute coordinated product launches that match investment level to strategic impact and generate measurable adoption within the first 90 days. Target: generate 500 sign-ups or qualified leads in the first 7 days of a Tier 2 or Tier 3 launch, achieve a defined revenue or adoption milestone within 30 days, and establish a repeatable launch process with explicit tier criteria that prevents both over-investment in minor releases and under-investment in flagship products.

---

## Core Principles

**1. Tier assignment follows strategic impact, not development effort.** 95% of newly launched products fail commercially (Harvard Business School study, approximately 30,000 product launches). A defined launch process yields 10% higher success rates and three times greater revenue growth versus ad-hoc approaches. The launch tier framework works because it forces the question: what is this product's actual market impact, independent of how long it took to build? A feature that took eight months to build might still warrant a Tier 1 launch if it serves a narrow segment. A feature built in a sprint might be a Tier 3 if it redefines what the product does.

**2. Positioning before channels, every time.** The most common launch failure pattern is jumping to "where do we announce this?" before answering "what is this product, who is it for, and why should they care?" Positioning drives channel choice, not the reverse. Every downstream asset, the landing page headline, the email subject line, the sales pitch, is easier when positioning is locked first. A launch team that aligns on positioning in weeks 1 through 3 will execute faster and more coherently on channels in weeks 7 through 12 than a team that starts with channel planning.

**3. 26% of total product sales occur within the first 90 days.** The window immediately after launch is the highest-leverage moment in a product's life. This creates a specific operational implication: the pre-launch build-up (waitlist, beta access, press embargoes, partner briefings) determines what is available to compress into the launch window. A launch that starts building demand at week 10 of a 12-week runway is leaving the first 10 weeks of the highest-conversion window empty.

**4. Launch day is the start of the campaign, not the end.** 80% of product launches in 2025 required significant post-launch changes due to market disruptions, according to the Gartner Group. Teams that planned for iteration, rather than treating launch day as the finish line, recovered faster. A 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day post-launch motion is not optional contingency planning; it is a structural component of every Tier 2 and Tier 3 launch. The teams that achieve the best 90-day revenue outcomes build the iteration plan before launch day.

**5. Tier 3 launches require CEO-level sponsorship.** Internal misalignment becomes external incoherence. A company-defining launch needs the CEO to be able to articulate the launch message in one sentence. If they cannot, the launch is not ready. This is not about executive communication training; it is a signal that the positioning is not yet resolved. A launch with unresolved internal disagreement about what the product does and who it is for will produce fragmented messaging across every channel, and fragmented messaging in the first 7 days of a launch is very difficult to recover from.

## Strategy Architecture

**Step 1: Assign the tier using the five impact questions**
Before any launch planning begins, answer five questions about the product or feature being launched. The more "yes" answers, the higher the tier: (1) Does it change purchase decisions—will prospects now choose you over a competitor because of this? (2) Does it expand the addressable market—does it open new buyer personas or industries? (3) Does it shift competitive positioning—does it change how you are perceived versus alternatives? (4) Does it create a new revenue opportunity—does it unlock new pricing, upsell paths, or deal sizes? (5) Does it require market education—is this solving a problem buyers did not know they had? Four or five "yes" answers is a Tier 3. Two or three is a Tier 2. One or zero is a Tier 0 or Tier 1. Document the tier assignment with the evidence for each answer.

**Step 2: Lock positioning and messaging before any asset creation**
Weeks 1 through 3 of a Tier 3 launch are positioning and messaging weeks. Define in writing: the primary buyer persona, the specific job the product does for them, the one reason they should choose this over alternatives, and the proof point that makes that claim credible. Do not write landing page copy, email subject lines, or press releases until this document is reviewed and signed off by the CEO or product leader. Every asset created before positioning is locked will need to be rewritten.

**Step 3: Build the pre-launch demand asset (waitlist, beta, or early access)**
The Notion AI launch in November 2023 generated 1 million users on the AI waitlist before general availability through a 10-week campaign that began with a waitlist. A waitlist serves three functions: it generates a demand signal that validates marketing spend before the full launch, it creates a day-1 activation pool that generates immediate usage data, and it builds press credibility (journalists can cite waitlist numbers as social proof). Open waitlists for Tier 2 and Tier 3 launches at week 4 or 5 of the runway, not the final week.

**Step 4: Execute the pre-launch content and press phase (weeks 7 to 9)**
Weeks 7 through 9 are for embargo briefings and asset creation. Brief major press contacts under embargo with a specific embargo lift date tied to launch day. Brief analysts separately with product data. Complete all launch assets: landing page, sales deck, demo video, email sequences, one-pagers, and social creative. Run internal sales enablement training during this window so the sales team can talk about the product before it is announced. Partner co-marketing activation should also be locked during this phase.

**Step 5: Execute the coordinated channel push on launch day**
Launch day is a coordinated, simultaneous activation across all channels: embargo lift for press, email to full subscriber list, social across all brand channels, paid media activation, in-product notification to existing users, and sales outreach to high-intent pipeline contacts. The key discipline: every channel should carry the same core positioning message (derived from Step 2) expressed in that channel's native format. Fragmented messaging across channels is the most visible sign of a poorly planned launch. Monitor coverage and social response in real time for the first 12 hours.

**Step 6: Execute the 30-60-90 day post-launch motion**
Days 1 to 30 post-launch: convert launch interest into qualified pipeline through rapid response content (FAQ posts addressing objections that emerged in launch coverage, sales calls with waitlist signups, case study interviews with beta users). Days 31 to 60: publish first user success stories and outcome data. These are the most effective content assets for sustaining post-launch momentum because they prove the product works for real buyers, not just in marketing copy. Days 61 to 90: assess adoption data against targets, identify the 20% of the audience that is generating 80% of adoption, and redirect messaging to that segment.

## Content Pillars

**Pre-Launch Demand Content: Teaser and Waitlist Campaigns**
Content designed to build an audience before launch day. This includes teaser posts that announce "something is coming" with a specific value signal (not just a vague tease), waitlist landing pages that communicate the specific benefit of being first, and behind-the-scenes content that builds anticipation without revealing everything. The goal is a day-1 activation pool of known, interested users who will generate early adoption data.

**Launch Day Content: Coordinated Multi-Channel Announcement**
The simultaneous activation package that hits on launch day: press release (for Tier 2 and above), launch email, social posts across all brand channels, in-product notification for existing users, and paid media creative. All content in this pillar carries the same positioning message expressed in each channel's native format. Landing page copy should answer the five most common objections raised by the target buyer within the first scroll.

**Post-Launch Proof Content: User Outcomes and Social Validation**
Case studies, testimonials, review solicitation campaigns, and outcome data posts that prove the product works for real buyers. This pillar is the most commonly skipped phase of product launch strategy and the most valuable for sustained post-launch growth. The 25% revenue increase within the first year documented by Deloitte (2023) for successful product launches comes substantially from the credibility built in the post-launch proof phase, not from launch day media coverage.

**Enablement Content: Sales Tools and Internal Alignment**
One-pagers, battlecards, demo scripts, objection-handling guides, and FAQ documents that enable the sales team to convert launch interest into pipeline. For Tier 2 and Tier 3 launches, the internal enablement quality is often the difference between a launch that generates press coverage and a launch that generates revenue. Sales enablement should be completed before launch day, tested in practice calls, and refined based on the objections that actually emerge in the first 30 post-launch sales conversations.

## Cadence

Tier 3 launches: 10 to 12 week runway. Tier 2 launches: 6 to 8 week campaign. Tier 1 launches: 2 to 3 week targeted campaign. Tier 0: same day or next day.

Recommended annual launch volume (from practitioners): 1 to 2 Tier 3 launches per year, 4 to 6 Tier 2 launches, 10 to 15 Tier 1 launches, dozens of Tier 0 updates. If the numbers look very different from this, tier assignments are probably off: either over-tiering routine updates or under-investing in strategically significant ones.

**Publishing Volume (Tier 3 Launch)**

- Pre-launch teaser content: 1 to 2 posts per week for 4 weeks before launch
- Waitlist landing page: live at week 4 of runway
- Press briefings: 5 to 10 outlets briefed under embargo in weeks 7 to 9
- Launch day content: email (full list), social posts (all channels), paid media activated, press release
- Post-launch content: 1 to 2 case studies per week for 6 weeks after launch

## Distribution & Amplification

1. **Build the waitlist before the launch.** The demand signal from a waitlist gives PR teams a credible "already X people waiting" number for press pitches, gives the ad team a warm retargeting audience for launch day paid activation, and gives product teams early signal on which use cases are most compelling to potential buyers. Open waitlists 4 to 6 weeks before launch day, not the final week.

2. **Coordinate the embargo lift for maximum press concentration.** For Tier 2 and Tier 3 launches, brief press under embargo with an embargo lift time that aligns with the general product announcement. Multiple outlets covering the launch simultaneously creates a concentration effect: the product appears everywhere on the same day, which drives search volume, social sharing, and the perception of market significance. Staggered press coverage generates lower aggregate impact than concentrated day-1 coverage.

3. **Activate existing customers as launch amplifiers.** Beta users and existing customers who have tested the new product are the most credible voices in launch coverage. Send them early access to announcement assets with a simple ask: share their experience on the channel where they are most active. One authentic customer post from a recognizable name in the target industry is worth more than ten brand-produced posts. Sourcegraph's Tier 3 launch for Cody included blog posts from the CEO and CTO alongside coordinated community posts from beta developers.

4. **Run paid amplification behind organic content that already performed.** Do not launch paid media on untested creative. Use organic social posts from the 48 hours before launch day (teasers, countdown content) as the paid creative seed: the posts that generate the most organic engagement are the ones most likely to convert with paid distribution behind them. This applies the same "earn before you spend" principle that drives Spark Ads performance on TikTok.

5. **Publish the post-launch performance review internally.** A structured 30-day post-launch review (what worked, what did not, what would be done differently at each tier) is the most valuable institutional knowledge a product marketing team can accumulate. 59% of product marketing and GTM teams rate their own launch maturity below 6 out of 10 (SHNO 2024 benchmark report). The teams that improve fastest are the ones that document and share post-launch learning across all future launch teams.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Treating every release as a Tier 3 launch.** When every update gets a press release and a paid campaign, audiences become numb. Press stops covering announcements. Customers start ignoring emails. Save the full launch investment for launches that genuinely meet the Tier 3 impact criteria. The discipline of tier assignment protects the attention budget of every stakeholder who participates in launches.

**2. Assigning tier based on engineering effort, not strategic impact.** A feature that took six months to build can still be a Tier 1 if it serves a narrow segment. A feature built in a sprint can be a Tier 3 if it redefines what the product does. These are different dimensions. Tier reflects market impact and strategic positioning change, not internal effort or complexity. Conflating the two leads to either burnout (over-investing in Tier 1 features) or missed moments (under-investing in Tier 3 products).

**3. Starting with channels instead of positioning.** Jumping to "where do we announce this?" before answering "what is this product, who is it for, and why should they care?" produces fragmented messaging that no channel investment can fix. The landing page, the email, the social posts, and the press pitch will all say different things if positioning was not locked first. Different things produce confusion, and confusion produces low conversion.

**4. Treating launch day as the finish line.** 80% of launches in 2025 required significant post-launch changes. The teams that recover fastest are the ones who planned the 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day post-launch motion before launch day. Post-launch case studies, review generation campaigns, and adoption-focused content are not afterthoughts; they are the mechanism by which launch-day interest converts into 90-day revenue.

**5. Running a Tier 3 launch without CEO alignment on the core message.** If the CEO cannot articulate the launch message in one sentence, the launch is not ready. Internal misalignment becomes external incoherence across every channel that requires executive participation: press interviews, investor communications, keynotes, and sales calls. Resolve the positioning dispute internally before the embargo briefing, not after coverage is live.

## Recommended Tools

- **Notion or equivalent:** Document tier assignment criteria, positioning, and launch playbook; use for the canonical positioning document that every launch asset references.
- **HubSpot or equivalent CRM:** Manage waitlist contacts, track conversion from launch interest to pipeline, and measure 90-day post-launch revenue attribution.
- **PR Newswire or Cision:** Distribute press releases for Tier 2 and Tier 3 launches; use for embargo management and journalist contact database.
- **Google Analytics 4:** Track launch day traffic spikes, landing page conversion rates, and referral source breakdown; essential for measuring the 7-day sign-up target.
- **Figma or Canva:** Produce the launch creative package (landing page mockups, social assets, email headers) consistently from the locked positioning document.
- **Loom or Wistia:** Record and distribute internal sales enablement videos (demo walkthroughs, objection-handling guides) before launch day so the sales team is ready on day 1.

## Adaptation for Your Brand

The tier framework applies universally, but the channel mix at each tier varies by product type and audience. B2B SaaS launches at Tier 3 are anchored by press coverage, analyst briefings, and developer or practitioner community activation. Consumer product launches at Tier 3 weight paid social, influencer partnerships, and retail channel activation more heavily. Both require the same positioning discipline and the same 10 to 12 week runway; what changes is where the day-1 attention comes from.

For brands with a large existing customer base, post-launch expansion to existing customers (upsell, cross-sell, or adoption campaigns) is often the highest-ROI channel in the first 90 days, not new customer acquisition. Only 40% of developed products ever make it to market at all, and of the ones that do, only 60% generate any revenue. The surest path to early revenue is converting existing customers who have established trust in the brand, not convincing brand-new prospects to adopt a product they have never heard of.

For brands launching in markets where they are not yet known, the pre-launch phase (waitlist, beta, press briefings) is the most important differentiated investment. Press coverage on launch day requires relationships built before launch day. Beta customers who will provide quotable outcomes on launch day require a beta program that began 8 to 10 weeks earlier. The brands that show up credible on launch day started building that credibility 10 weeks before the announcement.

Brands with limited launch budgets should concentrate their entire Tier 3 budget into the 7-day window around launch day rather than spreading it evenly across the 12-week runway. The concentration of impressions in the launch window drives the social proof and search volume spike that makes coverage feel like a market moment rather than a routine announcement.
