SUBSTACK ALGORITHM RULES (2025-2026) - READ BEFORE GENERATING ANY FORMAT
------------------------------------------------------------

These constraints apply to all Substack content. Violating them reduces subscriber acquisition and distribution.

1. NO EXTERNAL LINKS IN NOTES BODY — EVER
   External links inside a Note body suppress reach significantly. The Substack algorithm deprioritizes Notes with external URLs. The only acceptable link placement is in the first comment below your own Note, or in the pinned welcome Note on your profile. This is not a suggestion — it is the documented behavior of the platform as of 2025. Write the Note; put the link in the comment.

2. THE CONVERSION UNIT IS EMAIL SUBSCRIBERS, NOT FOLLOWERS
   Substack crossed 50 million active subscriptions in 2025 and paid out over $600 million to creators. The platform's algorithm is explicitly engineered to ask one question when deciding whether to distribute a Note: "Will this reader eventually pay for this writer's work?" Followers on Substack are nearly worthless as a business asset. Measure subscribers per Note, not likes. A 50-like Note that drives 30 new email subscribers beats a 500-like Note that drives 5.

3. RESTACKS FROM OVERLAPPING AUDIENCES ARE THE STRONGEST ALGORITHMIC SIGNAL
   When a writer in your niche restacks your Note, Substack interprets it as an audience match and pushes your content to their readers. A restack from an account with 10,000 subscribers in your niche is worth more than 50 original likes from random accounts. The operational implication: restack 5-10 Notes from larger accounts daily with a quote-comment. When they restack back, that amplification compounds. In late 2025, Substack began treating restacks as fresh content with a second distribution window 1-2 days after the original.

4. DAILY POSTING FOR 90 DAYS IS THE ENTRY COST
   Notes posters who publish daily grow up to 70% of their subscribers from Notes alone (Substack internal data, 2025). In a three-month window in 2025, 32 million new subscriptions were sourced entirely from within the app — with Notes as the primary driver. Creators who post weekly stall. Daily posters compound because each Note trains the algorithm on what their audience looks like. Creators who quit before day 30 almost universally report no results. Those who publish daily past day 45 almost universally report compounding growth.

5. FOUR PROVEN NOTE FORMATS — USE THEM IN ROTATION
   Contrarian take: "Everyone says X. Here is exactly why X is wrong in 2025."
   Vulnerable confession: "I earned zero from my newsletter for 14 months. Here is the specific mistake."
   Generosity Note: Publicly recommend 5-10 writers in your niche by @-handle with a one-line reason for each.
   Behind-the-scenes: Screenshot your analytics, your draft, your reading list, your process.
   The generosity Note drives the highest restack rates of any format — when a large writer restacks your recommendation of them, it can reach 50,000 new readers at zero cost.

6. WINNING NOTES BECOME NEWSLETTERS — WITHIN 7 DAYS
   Any Note that crosses 100 likes is a confirmed interest signal. Turn it into a full newsletter issue while the audience is warm and traffic is still building. Notes are not just discovery content — they are market research for your next issue. A newsletter issue that fails as a Note will probably underperform as a full issue too.

7. SUBSTACK OPEN RATES ARE 2X THE INDUSTRY BENCHMARK — PROTECT THAT ASSET
   Substack email open rates average over 45%, versus roughly 20% for the email industry overall (Substack, 2025). The subscribers acquired through Notes are the most engaged list you can build. Protect the quality of that list by publishing Notes that attract readers genuinely interested in your topic, not engagement-bait that draws clicks from people who will never open your emails.

FORMAT A: SUBSTACK NOTE (SHORT-FORM, FEED DISCOVERY UNIT)
------------------------------------------------------------

Use when: Daily feed content to drive subscriber growth, test ideas before a full issue, and build algorithmic distribution by appearing in the feeds of overlapping-niche writers.
Target: Under 300 words for the Note body. Hook must work in the first 2 lines before the "read more" truncation.
Best cadence: 1-3 Notes per day. Frequency is the entry ticket.

Required inputs: Brand | Author Name | Topic | Target Audience | Format (Contrarian / Confession / Generosity / Behind-the-Scenes) | Voice/Tone

Structure — follow these 4 sections exactly:

HOOK (Lines 1-2, standalone):
The first two lines must work as a complete thought that creates curiosity, tension, or a specific claim.
The reader must feel compelled to read more without clicking away.
Use the format that matches the input:
- Contrarian: "Everyone says [X]. Here is exactly why [X] is wrong in [year]."
- Confession: "[Specific failure or surprising outcome]. Here is what I learned."
- Generosity: "The [number] best [topic] writers on Substack right now:" (then list them)
- Behind-the-scenes: Name one specific metric, process, or piece of work visible in your screenshot.

[blank line]

BODY (Note content, under 280 words total including hook):
Deliver the specific insight, list, confession, or recommendation.
Be concrete. Use numbers. Name sources. Name people.
Do not hedge, qualify excessively, or end without a point.
Generosity Notes: list each @-handle with a one-sentence reason. Tag the handles so they see the Note.

[blank line]

CLOSE (1-2 lines):
End with either a specific takeaway the reader can act on, or a question that prompts a reply (not "what do you think?" — something specific to the topic).
Do NOT ask for likes or follows directly.

[blank line]

LINK PLACEMENT (first comment, never the Note body):
If the Note references a newsletter issue, a resource, or an external article: "Link in comments." Write the first comment text separately, formatted as: "[Resource title/description] — [URL]."

NOTE FORMAT CHECKLIST:
- Total word count under 300
- No external URLs in the Note body
- Hook works as a standalone in the first 2 lines
- Uses one of the four proven formats
- Ends with a point, not a generic question

FORMAT B: SUBSTACK NEWSLETTER ISSUE (FULL LONG-FORM ISSUE, 600-1,500 WORDS)
------------------------------------------------------------

Use when: Publishing a full newsletter issue. Notes should have been used to test the topic first — any Note that crossed 100 likes is a confirmed demand signal.
Target length: 600-1,500 words. Focus beats length. A 700-word issue that teaches one thing well outperforms a 2,000-word issue that covers five things superficially.
Substack email open rate benchmark: 45%+ average vs. 20% industry (Substack, 2025). Write to keep that premium.

Required inputs: Brand | Author Name | Topic | Target Audience | Key Insight or Takeaway | One Supporting Stat or Story | Voice/Tone

Structure — follow these 6 sections exactly:

SUBJECT LINE (email subject, 2 options):
Option 1 — Specific number or claim: "[Number] [audience] still do [specific wrong thing]"
Option 2 — Question format: "Why does [common belief] fail for [audience]?"
Target: 41-50 characters. The subject line is the only reason the email gets opened.

[blank line]

PREVIEW TEXT (1 line, extends the subject):
Continues the curiosity from the subject line. Never lets the email client pull a footer line by default.
Example pair: Subject: "The 3 metrics I stopped tracking" / Preview: "One of them surprised me most"

[blank line]

OPENING HOOK (first 1-3 sentences):
Start with a specific scene, a concrete number, or a named observation — not "In this week's issue, I am going to..."
The opening must earn the read. If the reader has to reach the second paragraph to understand what the issue is about, rewrite the first paragraph.

[blank line]

BODY (the core argument or insight, 400-1,200 words):
Teach one thing. Name it. Explain why it matters. Give one concrete example or case.
Use subheads every 200-300 words for scannability.
Include at least one specific stat with a named source and year.
Write in the second person ("you") more than the first ("I") — orient toward the reader's situation.
Short paragraphs: 2-3 sentences maximum before a line break.

[blank line]

CALL TO ACTION / UPGRADE NUDGE (1-3 sentences):
Free issues: One soft CTA at the end — "If this resonated, forward it to one person who would find it useful." Or: "Reply and tell me [specific question about the topic]."
Paid tier teaser: "Paid subscribers got the full case study [specific detail] this week. [Subscribe link text goes in comments or the end note]."

[blank line]

SIGN-OFF (author voice, 1-3 sentences):
Personal, specific, on-brand. Not "Best regards." Something that reinforces the author's voice and makes the reader feel they are reading a person, not a publication.

------------------------------------------------------------
FORMAT C: SUBSTACK THREAD (MULTI-POST CHAIN WITHIN NOTES)
------------------------------------------------------------

Use when: Sharing a topic too large for one Note but not yet fully developed enough for a newsletter issue. A thread is 3-7 connected Notes posted sequentially over 1-3 days, each designed to be a standalone but also to chain.
Algorithmic advantage: Each Note in the thread gets its own distribution window. Restacking Note 1 from a large account on Day 1 can drive new readers to Note 3 on Day 3. The thread compounds.
Target: Each Note in the thread: under 300 words. Total thread: 600-1,500 words across 3-7 Notes.

Required inputs: Brand | Author Name | Topic | Target Audience | Number of Notes in Thread (3-7) | Key Argument Arc | Voice/Tone

Structure:

THREAD OUTLINE (plan before writing):
Note 1: The hook — the claim, question, or observation that opens the thread. This is the distribution Note — it must be the strongest of the series.
Note 2: The evidence — one specific data point, story, or case that proves Note 1's claim.
Note 3: The complication — the reason the common approach fails, or a counterintuitive angle.
Note 4 (if included): The system or framework — the practical takeaway.
Note 5-7 (if included): Extensions, examples, or reader questions addressed.
Final Note: The close — a one-sentence summary of the full thread argument + a gentle subscribe prompt.

EACH NOTE IN THE THREAD:
- Hook (lines 1-2): Works as standalone. The reader does not need Note 1 to understand Note 3.
- Body: Advances the thread argument. Names the previous Note briefly if relevant ("Yesterday I shared X. Here is what that means in practice.").
- Link reference: If referencing the previous or next Note, name it in the body — do NOT include external URLs inside the Note.
- Thread marker: Last line of each Note except the final: "Next: [one-sentence tease of the next Note in the thread]."

THREAD SEQUENCING RULES:
- Post Note 1 first. Wait for restacks/replies (minimum 4-6 hours or until engagement peaks).
- Post subsequent Notes in the thread within 24-48 hours to maintain the distribution window.
- Restack the previous Note when posting the next one to keep it active in feeds.

------------------------------------------------------------
THREE CONTENT PILLAR GUIDANCE
------------------------------------------------------------

For a balanced Substack content strategy, rotate across these three pillar types:

PILLAR 1 - INSIGHT AND FRAMEWORK (Format A: Contrarian or Behind-the-Scenes, Format B):
The writer's original point of view on the topic. Backed by specific data, a named case, or personal experience. This pillar builds authority and drives restacks from writers in the same niche.
Example topics: "The one metric my best issue failed on," "Why the most-shared Substack posts violate every writing rule," framework breakdowns from real issues.

PILLAR 2 - GENEROSITY AND COMMUNITY (Format A: Generosity Note):
Publicly celebrating other writers in the niche. The generosity Note drives the highest restack rates of any Note format because it triggers a social norm — writers who are named feel compelled to restack the recommendation to their audience. One well-targeted generosity Note can reach 50,000 new readers at zero cost (Substack case study, 2026). Run once per week.
Example topics: "The 7 Substack writers I always open immediately," "@-handle recommendations in [niche] worth following this week."

PILLAR 3 - VULNERABILITY AND STORY (Format A: Confession, Format B):
Personal experience, failure, or counterintuitive discovery. This pillar drives the highest reply rate and the deepest reader connection. Replies from established accounts carry more algorithmic weight than likes — the Substack algorithm treats a reply from a writer with 10,000 subscribers as a strong audience-match signal.
Example topics: Specific failure stats from a past issue, an honest account of what stopped working and why, the cost of an early mistake.

RULES SUMMARY
------------------------------------------------------------

- No external URLs in any Note body — links always go in the first comment below the Note
- Notes: under 300 words, hook must work in first 2 lines
- Newsletter issues: 600-1,500 words, one core insight, 2-3 sentences per paragraph maximum
- The four Note formats must be rotated — no more than two consecutive Notes in the same format
- Every stat names its source and year
- Measure success by subscribers per Note, not likes per Note
- Subject lines: 41-50 characters, specific claim or question format
- P.S. equivalent for newsletters: sign-off is personal, author-voiced, specific to the issue
- SELF-CHECK: After drafting a Note, verify: Is it under 300 words? Does the hook work in the first 2 lines without reading further? Are there zero external URLs in the Note body? Does it use one of the four proven formats? If any answer is no, revise before outputting.
- BATCH DEDUPLICATION: If generating multiple Notes in one session, no two Notes may use the same format type consecutively. No two hooks may share the same opening construction. Each Note must advance a different point — do not repeat the same insight rephrased.

------------------------------------------------------------
FAILURE MODES TO AVOID
------------------------------------------------------------

1. EXTERNAL LINK IN THE NOTE BODY: The single most common mistake on Substack. Even one external URL inside the Note text suppresses reach significantly. The rule is absolute: links go in comments. If the Note references a resource, write "Link in comments" and draft the comment text separately.

2. OPTIMIZING FOR FOLLOWERS INSTEAD OF SUBSCRIBERS: A strategy that drives 200 followers and 5 new email subscribers is a failure on this platform. A strategy that drives 50 followers and 40 subscribers is a success. The whole point of Substack Notes is that every engagement can convert to an owned email address. Write for the subscriber conversion, not the like count.

3. POSTING ONLY ON NEWSLETTER PUBLISH DAYS: The creators who stalled after the 2024 algorithm update were almost all weekly-or-less posters. The algorithm needs daily signal to learn your audience shape. Posting once a week means seven days of no signal per newsletter cycle.

4. SKIPPING RECOMMENDATIONS: Recommendations is the platform's highest-ROI growth tool. Setting up 8-12 reciprocal Recommendations from writers in your niche takes 10 minutes and produces compounding subscriber flow passively afterward. Writers who skip it leave 30-50% of organic growth uncaptured (Substack case studies, 2025).

5. WRITING TWITTER-STYLE NOTES: Ratio-baiting, hot-take pile-ons, and engagement-farming hooks underperform because the Substack algorithm does not reward dwell time. It rewards restacks from accounts with overlapping audiences. The mechanism is completely different from X or LinkedIn. Write for restacks from relevant writers, not for general virality.
