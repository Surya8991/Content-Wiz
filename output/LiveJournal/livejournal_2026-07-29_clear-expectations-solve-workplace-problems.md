# Why Clear Expectations Solve Half of Workplace Problems

I keep noticing the same pattern when I sit in on postmortems. A project slips, a client complains, a team member feels blindsided, and everyone starts hunting for a root cause that sounds impressive: a skills gap, a broken process, a personality clash. Then someone finally asks the plain question. Did we actually agree on what we were trying to do? Most of the time, nobody did. We assumed we agreed, and that assumption cost us weeks.

I used to think good management meant solving problems quickly. Now I think it means preventing the fuzzy agreements that create those problems in the first place. Gallup's State of the Global Workplace report has tracked this for years: only a minority of employees say they clearly know what is expected of them at work. That single number explains more workplace friction than any org chart ever could.

## The Problem With "Done"

Two people can hear the word "done" and picture completely different outcomes. A manager pictures a polished deliverable ready for a client. A team member pictures a first draft ready for feedback. Neither person is wrong. They just never defined the word together.

I learned this the hard way early in my career, when I handed in a report I considered finished and my manager sent it back covered in comments. I felt defensive. She felt ignored. Neither of us had spent five minutes agreeing on what "done" meant before I started. A Harvard Business Review piece on team communication in 2022 made the point that most execution failures trace back to unclear success criteria, not lack of effort. That matches what I have seen in nearly every stalled project I have watched up close.

## Priorities Nobody Said Out Loud

Every team juggles competing goals: speed versus quality, cost versus scope, this client versus that one. Leaders often believe they have communicated priorities clearly because the priorities feel obvious to them. But obvious to the person setting the strategy rarely means obvious to the person executing it.

I once watched two departments argue for weeks about a missed deadline. It turned out each side had privately ranked the same three priorities in a different order, and nobody had ever said the ranking out loud. The Project Management Institute's Pulse of the Profession research has repeatedly found that a large share of project failure traces back to poor communication of goals and priorities, not technical shortcomings. Priorities that live only in someone's head are not priorities. They are guesses waiting to collide.

## The Context Nobody Actually Shared

Managers carry years of context about a client, a market, or a past failure. That context feels so baked into their thinking that they forget to pass it along. They give an instruction and assume the reasoning behind it travels with the words. It does not.

I have caught myself doing this. I asked someone to "keep the tone conservative" on a proposal without explaining that the client had been burned by an overpromising vendor the year before. Without that context, the instruction sounded arbitrary, and the result missed the point entirely. Shared history is not shared unless someone actually shares it, out loud, every time it matters.

## Direction Is Not the Same as Clarity

Giving direction means telling someone what to do. Giving clarity means making sure they understand why it matters, what success looks like, and how their piece connects to the whole. A manager can hand out plenty of direction and still leave a team confused, because direction answers "what" while clarity answers "why" and "how we will know." This is one of the core habits taught in [leadership communication](https://www.edstellar.com/category/leadership-communication-training) programs, and for good reason. It is a skill, not a personality trait, and it improves with deliberate practice.

I try to ask myself one question before I hand off any task now. If I disappeared for a week, would this person still know what success looks like? If the answer is no, I have not finished my job as a manager. I have only finished the easy part.

## Closing Thought

None of this requires a new framework or a longer meeting. It requires the discipline to say the quiet part out loud: what "done" actually means, which priority wins when two collide, what context shaped the request, and why the work matters. Half the friction I have watched teams struggle through traces back to one of these four gaps, quietly left open.
