# Adapting to Change at Work Without Losing Your Confidence

I noticed something last month when my team went through a reorg. Everyone said the right things in meetings. "Excited for the new structure." Then I'd catch the same people in the hallway, quiet and drained, asking each other what was actually going on. That gap between what we say and what we carry stuck with me, and I think it explains why so many of us feel like frauds during change even when we're handling it fine.

## The Gap Between Adapting Fast and Processing Slowly

Organizations reward visible speed. You get praised for picking up the new tool by Friday, for not complaining about the new manager, for jumping into the reorganized team without missing a meeting. But your nervous system doesn't move at the pace of a Slack announcement. Gallup's State of the Global Workplace report has tracked for years that employee engagement drops sharply during organizational change, even when the change itself is positive. That drop isn't weakness. It's the normal lag between an event and your brain's ability to file it away as safe.

I've learned to separate two things I used to treat as one: how fast I perform the new behavior, and how long it takes me to actually feel settled in it. I can run the new CRM competently within a week. I still might feel like a beginner in it for two months. Both are true at once, and neither cancels the other out. Managers who only measure the first one miss the second one entirely, and so do we when we judge ourselves.

## Rebuilding Competence Signals After Everything Shifts

A tool change or a role change quietly strips away your competence signals. You used to know which shortcut saved you ten seconds, who to ping for a fast answer, which report format your boss actually reads. After the shift, none of that muscle memory applies, and small hits to your confidence pile up faster than anyone expects.

The fix I keep coming back to is deliberately manufacturing small wins instead of waiting for the big ones. Finish one workflow in the new system end to end, even a trivial one, and notice that you did it. Write down the three things you already figured out instead of dwelling on the twelve you haven't. The Harvard Business Review has written repeatedly about how competence and confidence build through short feedback loops, not through waiting for mastery. You don't need to feel expert again. You need enough small proof points that your brain stops treating every task as a threat.

## Asking for Help Without It Reading as Weakness

This is the one that still trips me up. Somewhere along the way I absorbed the idea that asking a question in a new role signals that I shouldn't be in it. That belief is backwards, and I know it's backwards, and I still feel the pull of it every time I open my mouth to ask something basic.

What actually changes people's read of you isn't whether you ask questions. It's how you ask them. "I haven't touched this system before, walk me through the export step" lands completely differently than a vague "I'm lost." One signals that you're mapping unfamiliar terrain efficiently. The other signals distress. I try to name the specific gap instead of the general feeling, and it changes how people respond to me almost every time.

## Telling Change Fatigue Apart From Real Burnout

Not every heavy week is burnout, and treating it that way can make you distrust yourself during ordinary adjustment periods. Change fatigue tends to lift once the new normal actually becomes normal, usually within a few weeks of the dust settling. You feel tired, a little foggy, short-tempered, but a decent weekend still restores you.

The APA's Work in America Survey has flagged a different pattern for genuine burnout: exhaustion that doesn't respond to rest, a growing cynicism about the work itself, and a sense that nothing you do matters anymore. If a weekend off doesn't touch it, or if the flatness stretches past the point where the change itself has settled, that's a different conversation, and it deserves more than a pep talk about resilience.

I don't think the goal is to move through change without it rattling you. I think the goal is to notice when it's rattling you, say so to the right people, and keep functioning anyway. That's a much more honest definition of adaptable than the one I grew up believing.
