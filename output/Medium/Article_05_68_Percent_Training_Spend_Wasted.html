68% of Training Spend Is Wasted. Here Is the Diagnostic That Fixes It.

Most organisations fund training without first confirming what the actual problem is. The diagnostic step they skip is the one that makes everything else work.

In 2019, McKinsey & Company estimated that roughly 70% of corporate learning spending fails to produce measurable behaviour change. A more recent figure from the Brandon Hall Group puts wasted training expenditure at 68% of total L&D budgets globally. The numbers shift by study. The direction never does.

The reason is not poor facilitation. It is not inadequate technology. It is not unmotivated learners. The reason most training spend is wasted is that organisations commission training before they have correctly identified the problem training is supposed to solve.

The Skipped Step That Costs Everything

Training needs analysis is the diagnostic process that sits between "we have a performance problem" and "we will run a training programme." Done correctly, it identifies whether the gap in performance is caused by a skills deficit, something training can address, or by something else entirely: unclear expectations, broken processes, misaligned incentives, or inadequate tools.

The distinction matters enormously. A sales team missing its numbers because they lack negotiation skills needs training. A sales team missing its numbers because the CRM system produces inaccurate pipeline data needs a systems fix. Running a negotiation skills programme for the second team will not move the number. It will consume budget, generate completion certificates, and leave the actual problem untouched.

According to the Association for Talent Development's 2023 Skills Gap Report, 43% of L&D professionals report that training programmes in their organisations are commissioned without a formal needs analysis. In that environment, guessing right is a matter of luck, not strategy.

The organisations that get the most from their training budgets do not start with a solution. They start with a question: is this actually a training problem?

The Four-Part Diagnostic

An effective training needs analysis does not require months of consultation or an external firm. It requires four structured questions, asked in the right sequence, with honest answers from the right people.

What is the performance gap? Define it in observable, measurable terms. Not "the team lacks leadership presence" but "three of seven direct reports from this manager cohort have requested internal transfers in the past six months, citing unclear direction and inconsistent feedback." Specificity here determines whether the eventual intervention can be evaluated.

What is causing the gap? This is where most needs analyses stop too early. The immediate answer ("they don't know how to have difficult conversations") may be accurate. But it may also be a symptom. Go one level deeper. Do they lack the skill? Do they lack the confidence? Do they lack the time, or the psychological safety in a culture that penalises conflict? Each cause demands a different intervention.

Is training the right solution? If the cause is a skill or knowledge deficit, training is appropriate. If the cause is structural, such as an incentive system that rewards individual performance over team collaboration, training will produce no lasting change regardless of quality. Being willing to name this distinction is the most valuable thing an L&D function can do for a business.

What does success look like, and how will it be measured? Define this before any content is designed. The answer to this question shapes everything: the learning objectives, the evaluation framework, the post-training observation criteria, and the timeline for assessing impact. Without it, the programme has no fixed target and can never be proven to have hit one.

Building the Habit Into the Commissioning Process

The diagnostic is only useful if it is embedded in the process by which training gets commissioned. Organisations that run effective training needs analysis programs build this as a gate: no programme brief is approved without documented answers to all four questions. The gate does not slow the process significantly. It does dramatically improve the quality of what gets funded.

L&D teams that have implemented this gate consistently report the same outcome: a smaller but higher-performing portfolio. Fewer programmes, better targeted, with clearer evidence of impact. Budget that was previously spread across programmes of uncertain relevance gets concentrated on interventions with a confirmed business case.

Sixty-eight percent wasted is not an industry inevitability. It is the predictable result of funding solutions before diagnosing problems. The diagnostic is not an obstacle between the request and the training. It is the step that makes the training worth running.

Fix the front end of the process, and the back-end results fix themselves.
