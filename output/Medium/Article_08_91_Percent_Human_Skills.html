91% of L&D Leaders Say Human Skills Matter More Than Ever. So Why Are We Still Under-Training Them?

The consensus on what matters most has never been clearer. The investment patterns tell a completely different story.

In LinkedIn's 2023 Workplace Learning Report, 91% of L&D professionals said that human skills such as communication, collaboration, adaptability, and critical thinking are more important to their organisations now than they were two years ago. The figure has increased every year since the question was first asked in 2019.

In the same report, human skills training accounted for less than a quarter of average L&D budget allocation. Technical skills, compliance, and product knowledge continued to claim the majority of investment.

The gap between what L&D leaders say they believe and what their organisations actually fund is one of the most consistent and least discussed contradictions in the field.

Why the Gap Exists

The underinvestment in human skills is not caused by ignorance of their value. Every senior L&D professional understands that a technically proficient team that cannot collaborate, communicate under pressure, or adapt to change is a team with a ceiling. The gap exists for three more specific reasons.

First, human skills are harder to measure than technical ones. A cybersecurity certification either passes or it does not. A sales methodology either gets applied to the call structure or it does not. The impact of improved conflict resolution or more effective listening is real but diffuse. It shows up in team retention, fewer escalations, and better client relationships. Diffuse impact is hard to put on a budget justification slide, so it gets deprioritised in favour of training that produces cleaner evidence.

Second, human skills training is harder to design well. A technical skills programme can be built around a defined body of knowledge with right and wrong answers. A communication skills programme that actually changes behaviour requires experiential learning, skilled facilitation, psychological safety, and sustained practice over time. It cannot be delivered by an e-learning module with a quiz at the end. The resource requirements are higher, and many L&D teams are not resourced to meet them.

Third, many organisations still carry an implicit belief that human skills are either innate or the employee's personal responsibility to develop. This belief is wrong. The research on skill acquisition is unambiguous that communication, empathy, and adaptability are learnable and trainable. Yet it persists in cultures where leadership development is treated as identification of natural talent rather than systematic capability building.

What the Research Actually Shows

The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2023 lists analytical thinking, creative thinking, and resilience as the top three skills that will grow in importance fastest over the next five years. All three are human skills. The report projects that 44% of workers' core skills will be disrupted by 2027, and that the skills most resistant to automation are precisely those that require human judgement, relationship, and contextual reasoning.

The skills that are hardest to automate are the same ones organisations are least systematically developing. That is not a coincidence. It is an opportunity.

Deloitte's 2023 Global Human Capital Trends report found that organisations in the top quartile for human skills investment reported 24% lower voluntary turnover and 19% higher customer satisfaction scores than industry peers. The financial case is not ambiguous. What is ambiguous is how to measure the training itself, and that ambiguity is being used as a reason not to invest rather than as a design challenge to solve.

What Closing the Gap Actually Requires

The organisations making genuine progress on human skills development share a common approach: they treat these skills as a curriculum, not a workshop. A single communication skills session does not change how people communicate. A structured six-month programme combining workshop-based skill introduction, peer practice cohorts, manager observation and feedback, and real work application does.

This requires a longer investment horizon than most L&D programmes currently operate on. It requires measurement frameworks designed for behavioural outcomes rather than knowledge recall. And it requires leadership that believes developing human capability is as strategically important as deploying technical capability, which means it needs to be said out loud in budget conversations, not just believed privately in L&D team meetings.

The 91% who say human skills matter more than ever are right. The question is whether that belief will finally translate into the investment decisions that match it.

The gap between what L&D leaders know and what organisations fund is not a knowledge problem. It is a courage problem: the courage to make the case for investments whose returns are real but take longer to see, and to design programmes worthy of the skills they claim to develop.

The organisations that close this gap in the next three years will have a workforce that is genuinely hard to replicate. That is a competitive position worth building.
