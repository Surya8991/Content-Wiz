The Problem With SMART Goals and What Actually Works Instead

SMART goals are everywhere in corporate performance management. They are also quietly producing the wrong outcomes in most of the organisations using them.

SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) have been a staple of corporate performance management since George Doran introduced the framework in a 1981 paper in Management Review. Forty-five years later, the framework is taught in every business school, embedded in every performance review system, and referenced in virtually every manager training programme.

They are also, in many of the contexts where they are used, producing goals that are safe, small, and strategically useless.

What SMART Goals Do Well and Where They Break Down

The SMART framework solves a real problem. Vague goals like "improve team communication," "be more strategic," or "develop leadership presence" are impossible to evaluate and provide no clear direction for effort. SMART criteria force specificity and create the conditions for an honest performance conversation. For operational tasks with clear outputs and short time horizons, the framework works well.

The breakdown occurs when SMART criteria are applied to goals that are genuinely ambitious, exploratory, or transformational. The "Achievable" criterion is the primary culprit. When employees and managers know that goals will be evaluated against whether they were achieved, the rational response is to set goals with a high probability of achievement. The result is a performance management system that systematically incentivises low ambition.

A 2021 study by Gartner found that 87% of employees said their SMART goals felt "disconnected from the broader strategic direction of their organisation." The same study found that high performers, the employees organisations most want to retain, were the most likely to find the SMART process demotivating, precisely because it constrained the scope of what they could commit to pursuing.

A goal-setting system that demotivates high performers while rewarding conservative ambition is not a performance management tool. It is a talent retention risk.

What the Research on Effective Goal-Setting Actually Shows

Edwin Locke and Gary Latham's goal-setting theory, the most replicated body of research in industrial-organisational psychology spanning more than 400 studies, finds that the goals most strongly correlated with high performance are not achievable goals. They are challenging goals. Goals that require growth, that have a meaningful probability of failure, and that connect clearly to something the person doing the work actually cares about.

This finding is robust across industries, cultures, and role types. The SMART framework's "Achievable" criterion directly contradicts it. By design, SMART goals exclude the level of challenge that produces the highest performance.

The second finding from Locke and Latham's research is equally important: commitment to a goal matters more than its structure. A person who genuinely wants to achieve a goal, who finds it meaningful and chose it rather than had it assigned, performs significantly better than a person executing a well-structured goal they feel indifferent about. The SMART framework addresses structure. It says nothing about meaning or commitment. And meaning is what drives the discretionary effort that separates adequate performance from exceptional performance.

Frameworks That Work Better

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) address the ambition problem directly. The objective is qualitative and aspirational: something worth pursuing that connects to a larger strategic direction. The key results are measurable indicators of progress toward the objective. Crucially, Google, who popularised the framework, recommends that teams achieve roughly 70% of their key results. A 100% achievement rate signals that the goals were not ambitious enough. This inverts the SMART logic entirely and produces a very different conversation between managers and employees.

Learning goals versus performance goals is a distinction from Carol Dweck's research on mindset that has direct application to goal-setting practice. A performance goal focuses on demonstrating competence: "achieve a customer satisfaction score of 4.5 or above." A learning goal focuses on developing competence: "develop three new techniques for handling escalated complaints and apply them in at least five real interactions this quarter." For employees building new skills or taking on new responsibilities, learning goals consistently produce better long-term outcomes than performance goals, because they make growth rather than proof of existing ability the object of attention.

Narrative goals ask a different first question: not "what will you achieve?" but "what story do you want to be able to tell about this period of your work in twelve months?" This framing connects goals to identity and meaning in a way that structured criteria cannot. It is less precise and harder to score, and significantly more motivating for people doing complex, creative, or relationship-intensive work.

SMART goals are not wrong. They are incomplete. Applied indiscriminately, they produce a performance management culture optimised for the appearance of achievement rather than the reality of growth.

The question every manager and HR leader should be asking is not whether their goals are SMART. It is whether their goals are worth caring about. The answer to that question produces very different conversations and very different results.
