Creating an Inclusive Workplace: Strategies Beyond Gender Equality

Gender equality gets the most airtime in workplace inclusion conversations. The organizations that stop there are addressing one dimension of a multi-dimensional problem.

Over the past decade, corporate diversity and inclusion efforts have made measurable progress on gender representation at the top of many organizations. More women hold board seats, more organizations have committed to pay equity audits, and more leadership pipelines are being actively managed for gender balance. These are genuine gains worth acknowledging.

They are also, in many organizations, being treated as the completion of an inclusion agenda rather than as one dimension of a much broader one. The result is workplaces that have improved along one visible axis while leaving significant exclusion intact along others.

The Dimensions That Get Less Attention

Inclusion research consistently identifies several dimensions that receive less organizational attention than gender, despite producing equally significant effects on belonging, performance, and retention.

Neurodiversity is one of the most significant. Approximately 15 to 20% of the global population is neurodivergent, including individuals with dyslexia, ADHD, autism spectrum conditions, and dyscalculia, according to estimates from the National Institutes of Health. Many of these individuals bring distinctive cognitive strengths, including pattern recognition, sustained focus on complex problems, and unconventional problem-solving, that organizations benefit from enormously when they create the right conditions. Most workplaces are not designed for these conditions. Open-plan offices, back-to-back meeting schedules, informal communication norms, and performance processes that reward extroversion over output all create systematic disadvantages for neurodivergent employees that have nothing to do with their actual capability.

Socioeconomic background is another under-examined dimension. Research by the Social Mobility Foundation found that employees from lower socioeconomic backgrounds earn on average 17% less than colleagues in equivalent roles who attended more privileged schools, even after controlling for role, experience, and performance ratings. The mechanisms include access to informal networks, cultural fluency with middle-class professional norms, and confidence in self-advocacy. None of these gaps are addressed by diversity programmes that focus primarily on visible identity characteristics.

Age diversity rounds out the picture. The workforce now spans five generations in many organizations, from employees in their early twenties to those in their sixties and beyond. Ageism operates in both directions: younger employees face credibility barriers regardless of capability, and older employees face assumptions about adaptability that are rarely tested against actual performance data. Both patterns represent lost talent and organizational capability.

What Structural Inclusion Actually Requires

The distinction between performative inclusion and structural inclusion is important. Performative inclusion focuses on visible representation metrics and communication. Structural inclusion focuses on redesigning the systems, processes, and norms that determine who gets hired, who gets developed, who gets promoted, and who feels safe enough to contribute fully.

An organization can have a diverse workforce and an exclusionary culture simultaneously. Representation without belonging is a retention problem waiting to happen.

Redesign evaluation processes for bias. Most performance evaluation systems contain systematic biases that disadvantage people who do not match the dominant cultural profile of the organization. Structured evaluation criteria, blind review processes where feasible, and calibration sessions that explicitly surface and challenge pattern-matching in promotion decisions produce more equitable outcomes than awareness training alone.

Audit informal advancement mechanisms. In most organizations, a significant portion of career advancement happens through informal sponsorship, visibility in high-stakes projects, and access to senior leaders outside formal processes. These mechanisms systematically advantage people who share cultural background, communication style, and social context with existing leadership. Formalizing sponsorship programmes, actively distributing high-visibility assignments, and making mentoring relationships structurally available to everyone rather than just the informal favourites of senior leaders are the interventions that close this gap.

Build psychological safety as a measurable outcome. Inclusion without psychological safety is incomplete. Employees who do not feel safe to speak up, challenge assumptions, or be honest about mistakes are not fully included regardless of what the representation metrics show. Google's Project Aristotle identified psychological safety as the single strongest predictor of team performance across more than 180 teams studied. Measuring it, and treating its improvement as a leadership accountability, produces tangible results.

The Business Case Does Not Need to Be Made Repeatedly

McKinsey's Diversity Wins report (2020) found that companies in the top quartile for ethnic and cultural diversity are 36% more likely to achieve above-average profitability than those in the bottom quartile. The research on inclusion's connection to innovation, decision quality, and talent retention is similarly robust. Organizations that still treat inclusion as a values exercise rather than a performance strategy are ignoring evidence that is now over a decade old.

Gender equality in the workplace is important and worth continued investment. It is also not the whole of inclusion. The organizations that will attract and retain the full range of human capability available to them are the ones treating inclusion as a structural project that touches every system in the organization, not a communications initiative that highlights progress on one visible metric.

Expand the frame. The talent that benefits from a broader inclusion strategy is already inside most organizations, waiting for conditions that allow it to fully contribute.
