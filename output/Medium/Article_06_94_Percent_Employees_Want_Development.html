94% of Employees Want Development. Here Is Why Technology Alone Will Not Deliver It.

The appetite for growth is nearly universal. The gap between that appetite and what organisations actually deliver is where talent walks out the door.

LinkedIn's 2023 Workplace Learning Report found that 94% of employees say they would stay longer at a company that invests in their career development. That figure has held near that level for six consecutive years of the same survey. It is one of the most consistently replicated findings in the talent research literature.

It is also one of the most consistently ignored findings in practice.

The standard organisational response to this data has been to invest in technology. A new LMS. An on-demand content library with thousands of courses. A personalisation engine that recommends learning based on role and browsing history. The tools are real. The investment is significant. And in most organisations, the 94% still do not feel developed.

What Employees Actually Mean by Development

The gap between what employees say they want and what technology delivers is a definition problem. When employees report wanting development, they are not primarily asking for access to content. They are asking for something far more specific and far harder to automate.

They are asking to be seen. To have a manager who understands their strengths, notices their growth, and connects them to opportunities that match their ambitions. They are asking for stretch assignments that build real capability through real consequences, not simulated scenarios in a learning module. They are asking for feedback that is honest, specific, and delivered in a relationship of trust rather than as an annual performance review ritual.

A Gallup analysis of 2.7 million employee responses found that the single strongest predictor of whether an employee feels developed is the quality of their relationship with their direct manager, specifically whether that manager takes an active interest in their growth. No content platform replicates this. No recommendation algorithm substitutes for it.

Technology can deliver access to learning at scale. It cannot deliver the human relationship that makes learning feel like development.

Where the Technology Investment Goes Wrong

The error is not in deploying technology. On-demand content, skill assessments, and digital learning pathways are genuinely useful tools when placed in the right context. The error is in treating them as the development solution rather than as infrastructure that supports a development culture.

An LMS with 10,000 courses does not develop an employee. A manager who identifies that an employee needs to build commercial acumen, recommends three specific modules, sets a follow-up conversation to discuss what they learned, and then creates an opportunity to apply it: that is what actually develops an employee. The technology enables one step in a chain that is fundamentally human. When organisations invest in the technology and neglect the chain, the library sits underused and the employee still does not feel developed.

The utilisation data tells this story clearly. Towards Maturity's 2022 benchmark study of corporate learning technology found that the average employee accesses less than 4% of the content available to them in their organisation's LMS in any given year. The bottleneck is not supply. It is the absence of a human context that makes any specific learning feel urgent and purposeful.

What Bridges the Gap

Organisations that close the distance between the 94% who want development and the minority who report receiving it share three structural features.

First, they make manager capability in development conversations a non-negotiable part of what it means to be a manager in their organisation. Not a training module managers complete once, but a practised skill that is observed, coached, and included in how managers are evaluated. The manager is the primary delivery mechanism for development. Treating them as an optional variable in the system produces optional results.

Second, they build development planning into the operating rhythm of the business, treating it not as an annual HR process but as a quarterly or monthly conversation between manager and employee. These conversations have a structure: what are you working on, what are you learning, what opportunity should we be looking for next? Short, regular, and specific beats comprehensive and annual every time.

Third, they use technology to support these conversations rather than replace them. The right question is not "what courses should we make available?" It is "what information does a manager need to have a better development conversation with this employee?" Technology that answers the second question produces measurably better outcomes than technology that answers the first.

Ninety-four percent of employees want development. The number has not moved in six years because the gap is not a content problem. It is a relationship problem, a manager capability problem, and a culture problem. Technology accelerates what is already working. It cannot create what is missing.

Build the human infrastructure first. The technology investment will finally deliver on its promise.
