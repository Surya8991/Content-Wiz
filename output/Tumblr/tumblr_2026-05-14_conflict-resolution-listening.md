# Conflict Resolution Starts with Listening, Not Winning

Most people enter a conflict trying to win it. That is why most conflicts drag on longer than they need to.

The shift from winning to resolving requires one fundamental change: you have to understand what the other person actually wants before you respond. Not what you assume they want. Not what you want them to want. What they actually want.

This sounds obvious. It is surprisingly rare in practice.

**What listening in conflict actually looks like:**

It means asking clarifying questions instead of counter-arguing. "Help me understand what you meant when you said..." buys more resolution than "That is not what happened."

It means summarizing their position back to them before presenting yours. If you can accurately describe what the other person said in a way they would agree with, you have earned the right to present your perspective.

It means separating the person from the position. Someone can be wrong about a specific thing and right about something else in the same conversation. Treating them as entirely right or entirely wrong makes resolution nearly impossible.

It means tolerating silence. The discomfort of a pause does not require you to fill it with something that escalates the situation.

Conflict is not the problem. Conflict handled without the other person feeling heard is the problem.

The teams and relationships with the healthiest conflict cultures are not the ones with the fewest disagreements. They are the ones where disagreement does not require a winner.
